Single particle problem and solution

This comes from Landau Levels section in Shankar.

Consider a single particle of mass \(m\) and charge \(q\) in the \(x\)-\(y\) plane. Then imagine a magnetic field \(\mathrm{B} = B \hat{z}\) pointing perpendicularly to the plane.

(There is an exercise in Shankar’s book to fill in the details of the argument below. I will come back later to do so with either Python or Julia.)

Write the vector potential as \(A = B/2 (-y \hat{x} + x \hat{y})\). Then the Hamiltonian for the system is \[ H = \frac{(P_x + qYB/2c)^2}{2m} + \frac{(P_x - qXB/2c)^2}{2 m}. \]

Also, if we define the operators \(Q\) and \(P\) by \[ \begin{align} Q &= \frac{cP_x + q Y B/2}{q B} \\ P &= P_y - qBX/2c \end{align} \] then they satisfy the canonical commutation relation \([Q, P] = i \hbar\), and the Hamiltonian may be rewritten with these variables as \[ H = \frac{P^2}{2m} + \frac{1}{2}m \omega_0^2 Q^2 \] where \(\omega_0 = qB/mc\) is the cyclotron frequency.

We can define the annihilation operator \(a\) by \[ a = \left(\frac{m \omega_0}{2 \hbar}\right)^{1/2} Q + i \left(\frac{1}{2m\omega_0 \hbar}\right)^{1/2} P \] and the creation operator \(a^\dagger\), the adjoint of \(a\) (with respect to the \(L^2\) inner product most likely). With these the Hamiltonian \(H\) becomes \[ H = (a^\dagger a + \frac{1}{2}) \hbar \omega_0. \]

Shankar then points out that the Hamiltonian for a 2D problem reduces to a Hamiltonian for a 1D problem. This oddity of the dimension reduction can be explained by the presence of another pair of canonical variables \((Q', P')\), defined by swapping the \(+\) and \(-\) sign in the definition of the pair \((Q, P)\) above. So we actually have four operators \(Q, Q', P, P'\) with \([Q, P] = [Q', P'] = i\hbar\), which otherwise commute.

From the lens of quantum field theory, it just sounds like we’re talking about a 2D QFT whose Hamiltonian only cares about one of the coordinates.

Landau Levels

Sticking with that realization, it’s not at all surprising that we can index the eigenstates of \(H\) with two quantum numbers, perhaps \(m\) (oh no a notation clash… anyway, moving on) and \(n\), where one index, say \(m\), has no effect on the energy - it’s the free particle quantum number for the “direction” \(Q'\). The other quantum number, \(n\), corresponds with the energy levels of the harmonic oscillator that we reduced it to above: \[ E_{m, n} = E_n = (n + 1/2)\hbar \omega_0 \] The fact that the energy does not depend on \(m\) shows that each Landau level (indexed by \(n\)) is infinitely degenerate1.

The “manifold of states”2 that is the set of integers \((m,n)\) indexing the eigenstates of \(H\) is interseting to visualize. \(m\) is allowed to be any integer, while \(n\) is allowed to only be nonnegative.

Alternative explanation using complex analysis

There is another way to do this using complex analysis. (Or I don’t know, maybe it’s all the same somehow.)

Start by considering the complex variable \(z=x+iy\), with \(\bar{z}=x-iy\) being the complex conjugate (sorry physicists). The ground state(s), i.e. the \(n=0\) eigenvectors denoted by \(\ket{0}\), satisfy3 \[ a \ket{0} = 0. \] Let’s now expand this all the way back down to the original position and momentum operators, \(X\), and \(Y\).

To get to

\[ \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial \bar{z}} + \gamma z \right) \psi_0(z, \bar{z}) =0 \] where \(\gamma = qB/4\hbar c\).

With ansatz \[ \psi_0 = \exp(-\gamma z \bar{z}) u(z, \bar{z}) \] you can find that \(\bar\partial u = 0\), i.e. that \(u\) is a holomorphic function.

Hmm, so taking \(z^m\) as a basis for the space of holomorphic functions is what Shankar and others do. But what other “bases” might there be? Interesting. As a mathematician I would think to be a bit more careful about how we put an inner product or banach space structure on the space of holomorphic functions. Perhaps some of the odd things they keep saying about “fitting L3s into a disk” can be made more precise by solving a boundary value problem instead of the \(Q'\) “direction” being a free particle.
